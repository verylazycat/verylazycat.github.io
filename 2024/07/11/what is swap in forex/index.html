Foreign Currency Swaps Explained

A foreign currency swap is a financial agreement where two parties agree to exchange interest payments from loans denominated in different currencies.

This type of swap may also include the exchange of the principal amounts, which are then re-exchanged at the conclusion of the agreement. However, typically, the notional principal is used for calculating interest payments without actual exchange.

Key Points

  • A foreign currency swap involves exchanging interest payments on loans in different currencies between two parties.
  • It can include the exchange of principal loan amounts.
  • The two primary swap types are fixed-for-fixed rate swaps and fixed-for-floating rate swaps.
  • These swaps can provide companies with borrowing rates that are more cost-effective than those offered by local financial entities.
  • They can also serve as a hedge against the risk of currency exchange rate fluctuations for existing investments.

Understanding the Concept

The purpose of a currency swap is often to obtain loans in foreign currencies at more advantageous interest rates than those available in direct foreign market borrowing.

During the 2008 financial crisis, the Federal Reserve offered currency swap options to several developing countries facing liquidity issues.

The inaugural currency swap was executed in 1981 by the World Bank and IBM through Salomon Brothers, where IBM exchanged German Deutsche marks and Swiss francs for U.S. dollars.

Currency swaps can be set up for loan terms extending up to ten years and are distinct from interest rate swaps in that they may involve the exchange of principal amounts.

The Swap Process

In a currency swap, each party pays interest on the other’s loan principal over the agreement’s duration. At the swap’s end, any exchanged principal amounts are re-exchanged, either at the agreed rate or the current spot rate, mitigating transaction risk.

Currency swaps have historically been tied to the London Interbank Offered Rate (LIBOR), which is the average interest rate at which banks lend to each other internationally. However, the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR) is set to replace LIBOR as the benchmark in 2023. As of the end of 2021, no new U.S. dollar transactions use LIBOR, although it continues to quote rates for existing agreements.

Swap Types

There are two main types of currency swaps:

  1. Fixed-for-Fixed Rate Swaps: Involves exchanging fixed interest payments in one currency for fixed payments in another.
  2. Fixed-for-Floating Rate Swaps: Here, fixed interest payments in one currency are traded for floating interest payments in another, without exchanging the principal loan amount.

Reasons for Currency Swaps

Lowering Borrowing Costs

A common motivation for currency swaps is to access cheaper debt. For example, if a European company borrows from a U.S. company and vice versa, they can do so at rates more favorable than those available in their respective markets.

Mitigating Exchange Rate Risks

Institutions may also use currency swaps to mitigate the risk of exchange rate fluctuations, particularly relevant for companies with international business operations. By taking simultaneous positions in each other’s currencies, they can hedge against potential losses due to currency movements.

Purpose of Companies Undertaking Currency Swaps

Currency swaps are beneficial for accessing foreign currency loans at potentially lower rates and for hedging against foreign exchange risks.

Types of Foreign Currency Swaps

Swaps can involve exchanges of fixed interest rates or a combination of fixed and floating rate interest payments between parties.

Historical Note

The first recorded foreign currency swap occurred in 1981 between the World Bank and IBM Corporation.
