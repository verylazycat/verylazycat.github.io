Understanding “Repainting” in the Forex Context: Clarification and Examples

The term “repainting” in Forex trading describes the phenomenon where a technical analysis tool’s historical graphical representation is altered in response to incoming price updates.

A repainting indicator is one that updates its visual output as new price information, such as candlestick or bar charts, becomes available. A prime example is the Zig Zag indicator, which may revise its historical signals with the arrival of new market data.

The Downside of Repainting

The issue with repainting is the unreliability of the information it presents. Such indicators can create a misleading sense of precision in their displayed data. However, seasoned traders who are familiar with repainting indicators like the Zig Zag are aware of this behavior and often use these tools in conjunction with other forms of technical analysis, economic fundamentals, price action studies, or indicators that do not repaint, for validation purposes.

Reasons Behind Indicator Repainting

There are several reasons why an indicator might repaint, including:

  • Poor coding practices leading to unintentional repainting.
  • Deliberate design choices by unscrupulous parties to deceive traders into believing in the backtest results or promotional examples, potentially prompting a purchase.
  • The inherent limitations in coding that prevent an indicator from being non-repainting.

The Existence of Effective Non-Repainting Indicators

Absolutely, there are beneficial non-repainting indicators available. Renowned developers such as Mrtools and Mladen are known for their expertise in crafting indicators that do not repaint. It is advisable to seek out indicators from these credible sources prior to considering other options.

Detecting Repainting in an Indicator

To discern whether an indicator exhibits repainting behavior, follow these straightforward steps:

  • Position the indicator on a chart with a 1-minute timeframe.
  • Monitor the indicator closely to see if its signals or values are altered as fresh market data is integrated.

Upon the arrival of new price data, if the chart’s arrows vanish or if the Oscillator revises its previous crossings or angles after several candles have formed, these alterations are indicative of repainting activity.
