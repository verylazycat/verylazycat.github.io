forex trend patterns you must know

Created2024-07-09|Updated2024-07-09
|Word Count:778|Reading Time:4mins|Post Views:

We’ve covered a vast array of chart patterns for you, and we’re exhausted from the effort. But fear not, we won’t abandon you until you’re fully equipped to proceed on your own…

We’re just teasing! We won’t leave your side until you’re confident and ready!

In this segment, we’ll delve deeper into the practical application of these chart patterns to maximize their benefits for you.

Merely understanding the functionality of these tools is insufficient; we must also master their use. With these new additions to your trading toolkit, it’s time to ignite those profits!

Let’s recap the chart patterns we’ve learned and organize them based on the trading signals they indicate.

Reversal Chart Patterns

Reversal patterns on a chart are indicative configurations that suggest an impending shift in the prevailing trend’s direction.

The emergence of a reversal pattern within an uptrend is a sign that the trend is likely to invert, indicating an imminent decline in price.

On the flip side, spotting a reversal pattern in a downtrend is an indication that the price is expected to rise subsequently.

Throughout this lesson, we’ve explored six distinct chart patterns that indicate potential trend reversals. Can you recall each of the six?

  1. Double Top
  2. Double Bottom
  3. Head and Shoulders
  4. Inverse Head and Shoulders (also known as Head and Shoulders Top)
  5. Rising Wedge
  6. Falling Wedge

If you’ve correctly identified all six, well done! You deserve a commendation!

To engage with these chart patterns in trading, just execute a trade beyond the neckline, aligning with the direction of the emerging trend. Aim for a target that approximates the height of the pattern itself.

For example, upon identifying a double bottom pattern, initiate a long trade at the peak of the neckline where the pattern is formed, and set your target to a level equivalent to the vertical distance from the lows to the neckline.

To ensure prudent risk management, always remember to set your stop orders. A sensible stop loss placement would be roughly at the pattern’s midpoint.

To illustrate, if you’re trading a double bottom, measure the distance between the neckline and the lowest points, then take half of that measurement to determine the appropriate distance for your stop loss.

Continuation Chart Patterns

Continuation patterns on a chart are indicative of formations that suggest the existing trend is set to continue.

These patterns are commonly referred to as consolidation patterns because they illustrate a brief respite for buyers or sellers before they proceed in the direction of the existing trend.

Market trends do not typically advance in a linear fashion, either upward or downward. They experience pauses, move laterally, undergo corrective moves up or down, and then re-accumulate energy to resume the overarching trend.

We have previously discussed various continuation chart patterns, including wedges, rectangles, and pennants. It’s important to recognize that wedges can function as either reversal or continuation patterns, contingent upon the trend in which they emerge.

To engage with these patterns in trading, just initiate a trade either above or below the pattern’s boundary, aligning with the direction of the prevailing trend.

For wedges and rectangles, set a target that equals or exceeds the dimension of the pattern itself.

In the case of pennants, you may set a more ambitious target, equivalent to the length of the pennant’s vertical pole.

When dealing with continuation patterns, stop orders are typically positioned outside the pattern, either above or below it.

For instance, if you’re trading within a bearish rectangle, situate your stop slightly above the rectangle’s peak or the resistance level.

Bilateral Chart Patterns

Ambiguous chart patterns present a bit of a challenge because they indicate that the price has the potential to move in either direction.

Confusing, isn’t it? That’s the nature of a bilateral signal.

This is particularly true for triangle patterns. Recall our previous discussion about how the price could potentially break to the upside or downside with triangular formations?

When trading with these chart patterns, it’s important to contemplate both potential outcomes (a breakout to the upside or the downside) and to set up two orders accordingly—one above the pattern and one below.

Should one order be activated, you can then eliminate the other. Regardless, you’ll be engaged in the market movement.

It doubles your chances, doubling the excitement!

The challenge, however, is the risk of encountering a false breakout if your entry orders are positioned too near to the pattern’s peak or trough.

Thus, exercise caution and always remember to set your stop-loss orders as well!
